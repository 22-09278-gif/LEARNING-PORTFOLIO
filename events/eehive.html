EEHIVE: Electrical Engineering Innovators Conference 2026

EEHIVE was an online student research conference showcasing innovative studies in sustainable agriculture, smart power systems, renewable energy, electroculture, power system simulation, and solar-powered automation.

← Back to TimelineEvidence Gallery
Event Documentation

Official Poster and Program

The official EEHIVE poster and program flow document the conference schedule and the student research topics presented during the online session.

Main Topics / Projects

  • Automated coir pot production for sustainable agriculture
  • Solar-powered drip irrigation system for dragon fruit cultivation
  • IoT-enabled adaptive microcontroller-based three-phase power factor correction device
  • Comparative analysis of direct-current electroculture techniques applied to hydroponics
  • Design and implementation of a web-based power flow simulator
  • SPARTAS: solar-powered automated rodent trap and alarm system

Event Significance

This conference showed how student research can contribute to sustainable agriculture, smart power systems, educational simulation tools, and automated solutions for practical problems.

Report-Based Details

Expanded Learning Notes

Power flow simulation for education

The web-based power flow simulator focused on making power system analysis more accessible for students. It allowed educational and analytical study of power flow concepts through a web-based platform.

Smart power quality solutions

The power factor correction project emphasized that low power factor can increase current, losses, voltage drops, equipment heating, and electricity costs. Smart monitoring and adaptive correction can improve efficiency and reliability.

Sustainable agriculture and automation

Projects such as solar-powered drip irrigation, coir pot production, electroculture, and automated rodent control showed how electrical engineering can improve agricultural productivity and resource efficiency.

Knowledge Gained

Key Takeaways

1Agricultural innovation can be supported through automation, renewable energy, and smart monitoring.
2Power factor correction improves power quality by reducing losses, improving efficiency, and protecting equipment.
3Simulation tools make power system learning more accessible, interactive, and useful for students.
4Student research can produce practical solutions when supported by testing, validation, and clear problem definition.

Personal Reflection: EEHIVE helped me see student research as a bridge between theory and real innovation. The projects showed that engineering students can create useful tools for agriculture, education, energy efficiency, and automation. It also reminded me that validation and documentation are important parts of engineering work.

Application of Learning: I can apply this by valuing validation, testing, data-driven design, and user accessibility when developing engineering solutions. I can also explore more tools for power system simulation and smart monitoring.
