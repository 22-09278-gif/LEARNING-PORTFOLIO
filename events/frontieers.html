FrontiEErs: Foraging the Forefront of Engineering and Resilience

FrontiEErs connected engineering maintenance and operation with personal and mental resilience. The seminar showed that engineering success depends on both reliable technical systems and engineers who can manage pressure, uncertainty, and professional challenges.

Event Documentation

Official Poster and Program

The official FrontiEErs poster and program flow show the event sequence for the technical and non-technical panel discussions.

Main Topics / Projects

  • Engineering maintenance and operation
  • Pillars of maintenance management and asset reliability
  • Root cause analysis, motor control routines, and IoT equipment data
  • Mental health, stress, burnout, and emotional resilience
  • Financial and career dilemmas before licensure
  • Academic stress management and peer support

Event Significance

This event was significant because it treated resilience as both a technical and personal requirement. It showed that engineers must maintain equipment and systems, but must also maintain focus, mental health, and confidence.

Report-Based Details

Expanded Learning Notes

Technical panel: maintenance management

The technical discussion emphasized modern electromechanical maintenance, daily routines for motor controls, root cause analysis during faults, and the use of equipment data to support better operational decisions.

Non-technical panel: mental resilience

The non-technical discussion addressed the psychological toll of engineering studies, including fear of failure, parental expectations, financial pressure, board exam preparation, and the need to balance academics with rest and personal hobbies.

Community and peer engagement

The session encouraged participants to see that many students share similar struggles. The Q&A environment helped normalize conversations about burnout, confidence, and uncertainty in engineering education.

Knowledge Gained

Key Takeaways

1Maintenance is proactive; engineers must prevent failures, not only repair systems after breakdowns.
2Data improves reliability because monitoring, IoT equipment data, and root cause analysis support better decision-making.
3Mental resilience matters because engineering students and professionals face pressure, deadlines, financial concerns, and high expectations.
4Support systems are important because engineers work better when technical competence is balanced with well-being.

Personal Reflection: This event taught me that engineering resilience has two sides: reliable systems and resilient people. A good engineer must take care of equipment while also managing stress, uncertainty, and personal well-being. I learned that asking for support is not a weakness, but part of sustaining long-term growth.

Application of Learning: I can apply this by thinking preventively in technical work and by developing healthier routines, better time management, and openness to support when under academic or professional pressure.
